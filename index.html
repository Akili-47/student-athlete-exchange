The problem

The current system does not work for most athletes.

  • Internships don't fit the athletic schedule
  • NIL contracts reach a small percentage of athletes
  • Athletes need verifiable work experience before graduation
  • Athletic departments don't have professional development infrastructure outside the sport itself

The problem is not motivation. It is not capability. Every athlete competing at a high level already has the skills. The problem is that those skills are invisible — and every existing tool for building professional credibility was designed for people who have time.

The NIL problem no one is solving

Name, Image, and Likeness was supposed to change everything for college athletes. In practice, it changed everything for a small number of athletes — the most visible players in the most visible sports at the most visible programs.

The House v. NCAA settlement (2025) added revenue sharing and compliance pressure across all of Division I. President Trump's April 2026 executive order added further regulatory complexity. The result: athletic departments now face a professional development mandate with no mechanism to fulfill it at scale.

Athletes do not stop developing when their sport ends. They evolve — carrying forward the discipline, the competitive instinct, and the capacity for high-stakes performance that four years of elite competition builds. What they lack is a record of that development that any employer can open and evaluate.

The Exchange is the mechanism for the 99%. It produces verifiable work during the athletic career — not after it — so that when the sport phase ends, the athlete already has a body of work no other candidate in the hiring pool can match.

What The Exchange produces

Each session pairs a cohort of athletes with a domain expert — someone who has operated at a professional level in a target industry. Together they map the competitive landscape of a real partner organization.

Landscape

The athlete maps a real industry for a real organization — challenged by a domain expert against what actually happens, not what research claims.

Team

The session documents how the athlete contributed, what they challenged, and how they responded to correction. That is a more accurate picture of professional behavior than any interview question produces.

Results

The partner organization received the map and used it. What they did with it — the decision it informed, the strategy it changed — is documented in the portfolio entry.

The athlete does not say "I developed strategic thinking skills." They open a portfolio and say: here is the map I produced, here is who verified it, here is what the organization did with it.

That is not a conversation about potential. That is a record of results.

Four years of verifiable work

The athletic career is not a transition waiting to happen. It is four years of high-performance development that can run alongside a growing professional record. One session is a start. Four years is a body of work no other candidate in the hiring pool can match.

What makes this different

Every leadership program in sport trains athletes to execute inside a system they already understand. The Exchange trains athletes to read a system they have never been in before — and they leave with a record proving they did it.

That is not a refinement of what already exists. It is a different capability. And it is the one the transition out of sport actually demands.

For the athlete

You will practice being the person who does not know the room. Then you will have a co-signed record showing you figured it out anyway. That record travels with you into every interview, every application, every conversation about what you can do.

For the athletic director

This is not another leadership program. It produces a verifiable artifact — a map of a real industry, challenged by a practitioner, co-signed and version-controlled — that proves the athlete can read a domain they have never worked in. That is what employers actually hire for.

For compliance

Every session is co-signed, committed to a public repository, and auditable by any third party without a meeting. There is no liability in the documentation because the documentation is the product.

For athletic departments

The House v. NCAA settlement creates compliance pressure across Division I — including MEAC institutions operating with smaller budgets than Power Five programs.

The Exchange satisfies the beyond-NIL professional development mandate while producing auditable records that satisfy accreditation requirements — at near-zero operational cost.

One chapter replaces or supplements one career services function. The protocol costs nothing to operate once established. The first session is the proof of concept — no contract required to begin.

How this was built

The Exchange Protocol is not a pitch deck. It is a governance architecture built in the open — every document version-controlled, every decision traceable, every claim verifiable before a single conversation with a university takes place.

The methodology follows ArcKit — a structured governance framework that enforces documentation before each phase of work begins. The result is an architecture any institution can audit without a meeting, and any researcher can challenge without a gate.

The origin

I played football at West Point and Oregon State. I tried out for the San Francisco 49ers and got cut. After 18 years of football, I did not retire — I evolved. The identity shifted. The skills did not disappear.

I became a Ranger — The SeaBass, 1-Bravo, B. CO. 2/75, 2nd Battalion, 75th Ranger Regiment. I served in Afghanistan and was part of Operation Red Wings, the recovery mission for Navy SEAL Marcus Luttrell.

When I came back to athletics — as Managing Director of the OSU Athletics Leadership Institute — I gave athletes a problem to solve: how do you build real work credibility on a 34-hour athletic schedule? One team built the Student Athlete Exchange. It won first place at the 2013 OSU Startup Weekend.

That was the first answer. The Exchange is what thirteen years of field work revealed the answer actually needed to be.

— — Akili King, Lead Researcher
