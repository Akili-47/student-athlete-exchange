## What the Exchange is

An Exchange session pairs a small cohort of your
athletes with a domain practitioner — someone who
has operated in a specific industry at a professional
level within the last five years. The athletes produce
a Wardley Map of that industry. The practitioner
challenges every placement until the map reflects
operational reality, not published descriptions of it.
The signed map becomes a portfolio entry the athlete
owns and carries into their career.

An Exchange chapter is your institution's local
deployment of that protocol. One Chapter Lead, one or
more TACs, one Map Lead, and a cohort of athletes.

---

## Why it fits your institution

The House v. NCAA settlement creates a $20.5 billion
beyond-NIL professional development mandate across
Division I. MEAC institutions face this mandate with
a fraction of the Power Five budget — every compliance
investment has to do more than one job.

The Exchange satisfies the beyond-NIL mandate while
producing auditable records that also satisfy
accreditation requirements. It replaces or supplements
one career services function at meaningful cost savings.
The first session is the proof of concept — no contract
required to begin.

It fits uncontested space. Career services operates in
commodity space (resumes, job boards). NIL compliance
tools operate in product space (disclosure tracking,
marketplace management). None of them produce
domain-specific strategic analysis validated by
practitioners. That is where the Exchange sits.

## What you commit to

- **A named Chapter Lead** — existing institutional
  staff (athletic director designee, career services
  director, or equivalent). Approximately four hours
  per week.
- **A Map Lead** — institutional staff or trained
  student. Approximately ten hours per session,
  typically eight sessions per academic year.
- **One or more TACs** — practitioners recruited from
  your alumni or industry network. Volunteer role.
- **Three signatures on the Chapter Compliance
  Instance** — Chapter Lead, Map Lead, and TAC. That
  signed document is your chapter's compliance record.
- **A public, version-controlled repository** for
  session artifacts. GitHub free tier is sufficient.

---

## What you get back

- **A portfolio entry per athlete per session** —
  inspectable, permanent, employer-facing evidence
  of strategic thinking in a named industry, each
  signed by the practitioner who challenged it.
- **Replacement or supplement of one career services
  FTE function** at a fraction of the FTE cost.
- **Wardley Map deliverables for partner
  organizations** — competitive intelligence produced
  by your athletes under TAC supervision. Potential
  revenue stream through engagement fees.
- **Compliance-grade documentation** satisfying the
  beyond-NIL mandate and accreditation review — with
  zero additional documentation burden on your team.
- **Access to the TAC network** as a recruiting
  pipeline for athletes who have been challenged by
  sitting practitioners.

## What it costs

All figures are estimates pending institutional
finance and legal review. See the [Business Case](../001-chapter-mvp/ARC-001-SOBC-v1.1.md)
for detail.

- **Setup:** near zero. No platform licence, no seat
  fees, no software procurement.
- **New institutional spend per year:** $2,000–4,000
  (legal review — initial and annual). All other
  operating cost absorbed by existing roles.
- **University contract per chapter per year:**
  $15,000–35,000. Offset against existing career
  services FTE cost ($45,000–65,000 salary alone).
  Net institutional saving: $10,000–50,000 per year.
- **Partner organization engagement fees:**
  $2,500–7,500 per engagement (one per semester
  at MVP stage). These fees flow to protocol
  governance, not chapter operations — so the
  chapter is not dependent on them to run.

Do not cite any of these figures as negotiated terms.
They are planning estimates, not a term sheet.

## The timeline

Ninety days from agreement to Session 1:

- **Day 0.** Chapter agreement and participation
  agreement template reviewed by institutional legal.
- **Day 0–30.** Chapter Lead named. Map Lead
  identified. At least one TAC recruited and
  credentialed.
- **Day 30–60.** Athlete cohort onboarded.
  Participation agreements signed. Wardley Mapping 101
  guide and athlete briefing distributed.
- **Day 60–90.** Chapter Compliance Instance signed
  by Chapter Lead, Map Lead, and TAC. Session 1
  scheduled.
- **Day 90.** Session 1. First portfolio entry
  produced and committed to the chapter repository.

## What the Exchange is not

- **Not a platform sale.** No software licence. No
  subscription. No proprietary tool to procure.
  The governance architecture is open and MIT
  licensed. Your chapter signs an agreement, not a
  SaaS contract.
- **Not an NIL programme.** No compensation flows to
  athletes through the Exchange. No name, image, or
  likeness rights are exchanged. If an NCAA
  compliance officer asks: this is professional
  development output, not a benefit with monetary
  value attached.
- **Not a full career services replacement.** It
  supplements what career services already does. If
  you have a strong career services function, the
  Exchange extends it. If you do not, the Exchange
  may cover that ground at lower cost.
- **Not a founder-approval programme.** Your chapter
  is compliant when it signs the Chapter Compliance
  Instance. No approval from Akili King is required,
  offered, or meaningful. That is Principle 5 by
  design — the protocol governs, not the founder.

## Three things to do before you commit

1. **Read the [Business Case](../001-chapter-mvp/ARC-001-SOBC-v1.1.md).**
   Focus on the Strategic Case (problem and position)
   and the Financial Case (cost structure and
   sensitivity scenarios).

2. **Forward the [Data Protection Impact Assessment](ARC-000-DPIA-v1.0.md)
   to your university legal department.** FERPA
   exposure, GDPR-adjacent provisions, and the six
   identified risks are all addressed — your legal
   team can confirm by reading it.

3. **Identify one practitioner from your alumni or
   industry network who could serve as your first TAC.**
   Someone who has operated professionally in an
   industry your athletes care about, within the
   last five years. One TAC is sufficient for
   Session 1 proof of concept.
