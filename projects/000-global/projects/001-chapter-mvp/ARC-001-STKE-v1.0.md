## Purpose

This document maps every actor in The Exchange ecosystem
for the MEAC conference MVP deployment.

Each stakeholder is documented with:
- Primary goal
- Core driver
- Measurable outcome
- Tension point

This analysis is the basis for every university pitch,
partner organization conversation, and TAC recruitment
in the MEAC context.

---

## STAKEHOLDER 1 — MEAC Student Athletes

**Who they are:**
Student athletes competing in the Mid-Eastern Athletic
Conference — predominantly HBCU institutions including
Howard, Morgan State, Hampton, Florida A&M, Norfolk State,
and North Carolina A&T.

**Primary goal:**
A verifiable professional portfolio that demonstrates
strategic thinking and domain expertise to employers —
distinct from athletic performance credentials.

**Core driver:**
Most MEAC athletes do not go professional in their sport.
The athletic identity ends at graduation. What exists
after that identity is what The Exchange builds.
NIL income is temporary. A portfolio is permanent.

**Measurable outcome:**
At graduation, the athlete presents a documented record
of strategic analysis sessions — including assumption
corrections validated by domain experts — that an
employer can inspect, not just read about.

Employer response changes: from "tell me about yourself"
to "walk me through this map."

**Tension point:**
Time. MEAC athletes carry full athletic and academic loads.
The Exchange session format cannot add friction.
The ten-minute ETU close and structured phases exist
specifically to contain time cost.

**NIL-specific note:**
NIL income creates tax, contract, and brand complexity
that most MEAC athletes navigate without professional
guidance. The Exchange NIL domain track maps this
landscape — who the real players are, where the
value actually sits, what moves are available.
That map is more valuable than any NIL workshop.

## STAKEHOLDER 2 — TAC (Sports Business / NIL Domain)

**Who they are:**
Professionals with ten or more years of operating
experience in sports business, NIL marketplaces,
brand partnerships, athlete representation, or
sports marketing. Not academics. Practitioners.

People who know what actually happens in a
brand negotiation — not what the press release says.

**Primary goal:**
Contribute domain expertise in a structured format
that produces a documented evaluation record —
and surfaces pre-vetted talent they can hire,
refer, or mentor after graduation.

**Core driver:**
Every sports business professional talks about
giving back to athletes. The Exchange gives them
a mechanism that produces verifiable output
rather than informal mentorship that leaves
no record.

**Measurable outcome:**
After four years of evaluating an athlete's
mapping sessions, the TAC holds the most
credible professional reference that athlete
has — based on documented work product,
not personal impression.

The TAC also receives one Wardley Map deliverable
per engagement that reflects actual competitive
intelligence in their domain.

**Tension point:**
Time commitment versus benefit clarity.
TACs are busy. The session format is structured —
four phases, defined role, defined time boundary.
The TAC pre-work (segmented canvas setup) happens
before athletes arrive. Session evaluation is
two hours maximum.

**NIL-specific qualification criteria:**
TAC must have operated in NIL marketplace,
athlete representation, brand partnership,
or sports marketing at a professional level
within the last five years.
College NIL coordinator experience qualifies.
Law firm sports practice qualifies.
Brand-side athlete marketing qualifies.

## STAKEHOLDER 3 — MEAC Athletic Departments

**Who they are:**
Athletic directors, compliance officers, and
career development staff at MEAC member institutions.

Operating under the same House v. NCAA settlement
pressure as Power Five programs — with significantly
smaller compliance budgets and staff.

**Primary goal:**
Auditable, documented professional development
programming that satisfies accreditation requirements
and demonstrates beyond-NIL athlete support.

**Core driver:**
House v. NCAA created a $20.5 billion compliance
obligation across Division I. MEAC institutions
cannot absorb this the way SEC or Big Ten programs
can. Every dollar of compliance infrastructure
must do more than one job.

The Exchange replaces a career services function
with a protocol that costs near zero to operate
and produces verifiable output accreditors can inspect.

**Measurable outcome:**
- One career services FTE function replaced or
  supplemented at near-zero cost
- Documented professional development record
  per athlete, auditable on request
- Employer engagement channel through
  partner organization relationships
- NIL compliance gap addressed through
  structured domain education, not workshops

**Tension point:**
NCAA compliance uncertainty. The Exchange
positions portfolio entries as professional
development output — not NIL deals. This
distinction is documented in the SOBC
(ARC-001-SOBC-v1.0.md) and must be reviewed
by institutional legal counsel before deployment.

**HBCU-specific note:**
MEAC institutions have produced
a disproportionate share of Black professionals
across every industry. The Exchange is infrastructure
for that legacy — a structured protocol that
connects MEAC athletes to domain experts who
look like them and have operated at the highest
levels of their fields.

## STAKEHOLDER 4 — Partner Organizations

**Who they are:**
Sports business firms, NIL marketplaces, brands
with athlete partnership programs, sports agencies,
and media companies operating in or adjacent to
the MEAC athlete ecosystem.

**Primary goal:**
Strategic clarity on their competitive landscape
at below-market cost — delivered as a Wardley Map
produced by athletes under TAC supervision.

**Core driver:**
A Wardley Map produced by a group of athletes
who have mapped the NIL landscape from the
athlete's perspective — with assumption corrections
from a TAC who operates inside the industry —
is intelligence that no consulting firm produces.
It reflects what athletes actually experience,
not what brands think athletes experience.

**Measurable outcome:**
- One published Wardley Map of their
  competitive landscape per engagement
- Real strategic intelligence on where
  the NIL market is moving
- Direct access to a pipeline of athletes
  who have studied their domain specifically
- Brand association with HBCU athlete
  professional development

**Tension point:**
Trust in athlete-produced deliverable.
The TAC qualification and challenge process
is the quality gate. The governance documentation
at this repository is the credibility signal.
An athletic director pointing a general counsel
to this repository closes the trust gap faster
than any sales conversation.

## STAKEHOLDER 5 — Military / Service Members (Secondary)

**Who they are:**
Active duty service members and veterans
transitioning out of military service —
particularly those with HBCU backgrounds
or MEAC institutional connections.

**Primary goal:**
A professional portfolio that exists at
separation date — built during service
using garrison time and off-duty hours.

**Core driver:**
Military transition produces the same problem
as athletic transition: an identity tied to
a role that ends on a specific date, with
no portfolio of civilian professional work
to show for years of high-performance operation.

**Measurable outcome:**
Portfolio at separation = career corpus,
not entry-level proof. The veteran presents
documented strategic analysis — with domain
expert validation — not just a DD-214
and a list of acronyms.

**Tension point:**
Classification boundary. All mapped domains
are civilian organizations. No classified
information enters the mapping process.
Individual enrollment means no institutional
friction with unit commands.
