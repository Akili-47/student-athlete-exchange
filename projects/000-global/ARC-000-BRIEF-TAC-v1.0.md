## What this is

A one-page briefing for practitioners considering
a TAC role. Fifteen minutes. No quiz. No follow-up.

---

## What the Exchange is

An Exchange session puts a small cohort of college
athletes in a room with someone who has operated
in a specific industry at a professional level.
The athletes produce a Wardley Map of that industry.
You challenge every placement until the map matches
what actually happens on the floor.

The gap between what research says and what you
know from having been there is the learning artifact.
That gap is the reason the athletes are in the
room with you, and not reading a case study.

---

## Who the TAC is

TAC stands for Tactical Advising Coach.

You qualify for this role if you have operated
in the industry you will challenge, at a
professional level, within the last five years.
Academic knowledge does not qualify. Historical
knowledge does not qualify. What the athletes need
is current operational reality — not published
descriptions of it.

You are a challenger, not a facilitator. Facilitators
guide process. Challengers test assumptions. The
Map Lead handles process. You handle challenge.

The role draws on the British Army's doctrine of
constructive challenge — the responsibility of
capable people to push back when the direction is
off. You are expected to do that in every session.
The athletes are expected to push back on you.
A one-sided conversation is not a session working.

---

## What you commit to

- **Per session: about four hours.** Pre-session
  canvas setup (named user, domain boundary, known
  assumption traps) plus the session itself (three
  to four hours) plus co-signing the session record
  at close.
- **Per chapter, per year: two to four sessions.**
  You can TAC for multiple chapters. The commitment
  is per session, not a standing obligation to a
  specific athlete.
- **Domain currency maintained.** You keep operating
  in the field, or stay within the five-year window.
  When currency lapses, you step aside until it is
  refreshed.
- **Constructive challenge.** You push back on
  placements that do not match operational reality.
  You explain why. You accept the athlete pushing
  back on you. The session is a real conversation
  between a domain practitioner and a serious learner.

---

## What you get back

- **A pre-vetted talent pipeline.** The athletes
  who challenge your maps are people who think
  carefully, take correction, and produce work
  that reflects reality. You get to watch them
  think before anyone else does.
- **Documented professional reputation.** Every
  session you run leaves a signed record with your
  name on it. Over time, that record is evidence
  of your practitioner status to anyone who reads it.
- **Articulation practice.** Challenging placements
  against what you know forces you to state what
  you know, out loud, in a way another person can
  use. That is a skill that compounds.
- **Impact.** The delta between research and
  reality is what the athlete carries into every
  job they take for the next thirty years.
  You put it there.

## What the session looks like

Four phases, plus pre-work and close:

- **Pre-session.** You set the canvas — named user
  at the top, domain boundary, known assumption
  traps (places where research commonly misrepresents
  reality in your field).
- **Phase 1 — Open capture.** Athletes contribute
  components without filter. You do not correct yet.
  You watch what gets named and what gets missed.
- **Phase 2 — Theme clustering.** Athletes cluster
  components. You challenge every cluster — why is
  this in, why is that out?
- **Phase 3 — Value chain.** The chain must trace
  to the named user. You enforce it. If a component
  cannot be traced, it is removed.
- **Phase 4 — Evolution positioning.** You challenge
  placements against operational reality. Where
  research says "product," you may know it is
  still "custom." This is where the assumption
  delta lives. This is the core of the session.
- **Close.** You and the Map Lead co-sign the
  session record in SCQA format (Situation,
  Complication, Question, Answer). The signed
  record is the permanent artifact.

---

## What you are not

- **Not a mentor.** You are engaged per session,
  not as an ongoing advisor to a specific athlete.
- **Not a teacher.** You do not lecture. You
  challenge. Athletes learn by defending their
  placements, not by hearing yours.
- **Not a career coach.** The athlete owns their
  career. You tell them what is true in the
  industry — not what they should do with it.
- **Not compensated.** The TAC role is volunteer
  at MVP deployment. As the protocol scales, a
  TAC certification layer may become fee-bearing
  (see the Business Case, Commercial Case, Stream 3).
  At chapter 001 — MEAC MVP — no compensation flows.

If any of those are what you are looking for,
say so — we will point you to the right program.

## Three things to do before your first session

1. **Read the Wardley Mapping 101 guide.** Fifteen
   minutes. Plain language. Built by the community.  
   https://www.wardleymaps.com/guides/wardley-mapping-101

2. **Define your canvas.** Write down the named
   user at the top, the domain boundary (what is
   in scope), and three known assumption traps —
   places where published descriptions of your
   field are wrong or misleading.

3. **Come ready to challenge, not teach.** The
   session works when you stay in the challenger
   role. If you find yourself explaining how things
   work instead of pushing back on how the athletes
   described them — the session has drifted.
