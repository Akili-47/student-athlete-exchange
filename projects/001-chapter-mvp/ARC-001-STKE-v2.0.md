## 1. Power / Interest Mapping

The deployment environment contains both direct participants (Primary) and structural gatekeepers (Secondary). They are mapped here by their ability to influence the deployment (Power) and their stake in the outcome (Interest).

### High Power / High Interest (Manage Closely)
*These actors can veto the deployment and have a direct stake in its operation.*
- **Athletic Directors (MEAC):** Hold the deployment authority. Need auditable programming; fear compliance risk.
- **Compliance Officers:** Hold the regulatory veto. Need clear separation between professional development and NIL inducement.
- **Employers (End Users):** Hold the ultimate validation power. If they do not value the Wardley Map artifact, the protocol fails its primary promise to the athlete.

### High Power / Low Interest (Keep Satisfied)
*These actors do not participate daily but set the structural boundaries.*
- **Institutional Legal Counsel:** Do not care about athlete development; care entirely about institutional liability and House v. NCAA settlement exposure.
- **NCAA Enforcement:** Do not participate, but monitor Bylaw 12 (Amateurism) and Bylaw 16 (Extra Benefits) compliance.
- **SACSCOC Accreditors:** Do not participate, but audit the institution's compliance with student support service requirements (Standard 12.1).

### Low Power / High Interest (Keep Informed)
*These actors do the work and reap the benefits, but do not control the deployment.*
- **Student Athletes (MEAC):** The core beneficiaries. High interest in the portfolio, but low institutional power to mandate the program.
- **TACs (Domain Experts):** The operational engine. High interest in the talent pipeline, but rely on the institution for access.
- **Partner Organizations:** The intelligence beneficiaries. High interest in the map output, but operate at the invitation of the chapter.

### Low Power / Low Interest (Monitor)
- **General Student Body:** Not involved, but may raise equity questions if athletic resources appear disproportionate (Title IX/institutional equity).

## 2. Primary Stakeholders (Direct Participants)

### 2.1 Employers (The End User)
**Who they are:** Hiring managers, recruiters, and strategic leaders in target industries.
**Primary goal:** Signal of actual judgment and strategic capability, not just athletic discipline.
**Core driver:** Resumes and interviews are low-fidelity signals. Employers want to see how a candidate reasons through a problem before hiring them.
**Engagement strategy:** Do not pitch "athlete leadership." Present the Wardley Map as a documented work sample of strategic analysis in their specific domain.

### 2.2 MEAC Student Athletes
**Who they are:** Athletes navigating the transition from sport, operating under high time constraints.
**Primary goal:** A verifiable professional portfolio that proves domain capability.
**Core driver:** The athletic identity ends at graduation. The portfolio is permanent.
**Engagement strategy:** Focus on the artifact. "You will leave with a co-signed record showing you figured it out. You will be the only person in the room who can show their reasoning, not just claim it."

### 2.3 TACs (Sports Business / NIL Domain)
**Who they are:** Current practitioners with 10+ years of operating experience. Not academics.
**Primary goal:** Pre-vetted talent pipeline and structured, verifiable mentorship.
**Core driver:** Desire to give back, constrained by time and aversion to unstructured "coffee chats."
**Engagement strategy:** Emphasize the boundary. "Two hours maximum. Defined role. You correct their assumptions based on what actually happens in the room."

### 2.4 Partner Organizations
**Who they are:** Firms operating in the mapped domain (e.g., NIL marketplaces, agencies).
**Primary goal:** Strategic intelligence (the map) and early access to talent.
**Core driver:** Getting a landscape map built by athletes, corrected by a practitioner, is intelligence no consulting firm produces.
**Engagement strategy:** Pitch the intelligence value first, the talent pipeline second. "See your industry mapped from the athlete's perspective, corrected by a veteran operator."

## 3. Secondary Stakeholders (Structural Gatekeepers)

### 3.1 MEAC Athletic Directors
**Who they are:** Institutional leaders managing tight budgets under new settlement pressures.
**Primary goal:** High-impact, auditable programming at near-zero operational cost.
**Core driver:** House v. NCAA creates massive compliance obligations. Every dollar must do more than one job.
**Engagement strategy:** "Employers hire for judgment. The Exchange produces a verifiable record of it. It satisfies your beyond-NIL mandate at zero marginal cost."

### 3.2 Compliance Officers & Legal Counsel
**Who they are:** The risk managers. They hold the veto.
**Primary goal:** Zero NCAA violations; zero institutional liability.
**Core driver:** Fear of Bylaw 16 (Extra Benefits) violations or misclassified NIL activity.
**Engagement strategy:** Lead with the governance architecture. "Every session is co-signed, version-controlled, and publicly auditable. The documentation is the product."

## 4. Regulatory & Compliance Boundaries

The deployment must navigate three specific regulatory frameworks. This is what the Compliance Officer needs to see.

**1. NCAA Bylaw 16.3 (Academic and Other Support Services)**
- *The Rule:* Institutions are required to provide career counseling and life skills programming.
- *The Application:* The Exchange is classified strictly under Bylaw 16.3 as permissible career counseling and professional development. It is not an NIL inducement.

**2. NCAA Bylaw 12 (Amateurism)**
- *The Rule:* Athletes cannot be compensated for athletic skill outside permitted NIL structures.
- *The Application:* The Wardley Map portfolio is an academic/professional work product, not an athletic service. The Athlete Participation Agreement explicitly separates this from athletic performance.

**3. SACSCOC Accreditation (Standard 12.1 - Student Support Services)**
- *The Rule:* Institutions must provide appropriate student support programs that promote student learning and enhance development.
- *The Application:* The Exchange provides auditable artifacts (the ETU records) that serve as direct evidence of institutional compliance with Standard 12.1 for the student-athlete population.
