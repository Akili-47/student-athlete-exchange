> **In plain language.** What you are about to do, why you are doing it, and what you get out of it. Read this before your first session.

## The Problem

You are about to graduate with two things: an athletic credential and an academic transcript. 

Neither of those documents answers the question an employer actually cares about: *Can this person think strategically about my industry?*

Every candidate in the hiring pool claims to be a strategic thinker. They all have resumes that tell a story. When you walk into an interview, you are relying on a story to convince an employer you understand their business. The employer can see the whole board; you cannot. You will lose that conversation every time, and you will not understand why.

## The Solution

The Exchange Protocol gives you the board before the game starts. 

You are not going to listen to a motivational speaker or take a leadership quiz. You are going to build a map of a real industry, and then you are going to defend it against someone who actually works there.

When you finish, you will have a verifiable record showing exactly how you reasoned through a complex problem under pressure. You will be the only person in the hiring pool who can *show* their judgment, not just claim it.

---

## How It Works

### 1. The Pre-Work (The Map)
Before the session, you will research a specific industry or company and build a Wardley Map. A Wardley Map is a visual picture of how value moves from raw components to the end user. You will place components based on what you find in public research.

### 2. The Session (The Challenge)
You will present your map to a TAC (Tactical Advisory Challenger). The TAC is a veteran operator who has worked in the industry you just mapped within the last five years. 

They are not there to be nice. They are there to tell you where your assumptions are wrong based on what actually happens in the room. They will challenge your component placements. You will defend them or adjust them.

### 3. The Capture (The ETU)
In the last ten minutes of the session, you and the Map Lead will capture the **Execution Trace Unit (ETU)**. This is a short record of what happened:
- **Situation:** What was the map trying to show?
- **Complication:** Where did the TAC challenge your assumptions?
- **Question:** What was the core strategic question that emerged?
- **Answer:** How did the map change based on the TAC's operational reality?

### 4. The Portfolio (The Proof)
The TAC co-signs the ETU. The record is committed to a public repository. You now have a permanently verifiable artifact that proves you can read an industry you have never worked in, accept new intelligence, and adjust your strategy in real time.

### 5. The Rotation (The Arc)
You do not do the same thing every session. Across the program, you will rotate through different cognitive roles. In one session, you will be the **Pioneer**, mapping an industry with no playbook. In the next, you will be the **Town Planner**, running the room as the Map Lead and enforcing strict logic. You practice reading the map, and you practice defending the map.

---

## The Rules of Engagement

1. **You own the work.** The university does not own your map. The TAC does not own your map. You own it. 
2. **You do not need to be an expert.** The point is not to build a perfect map before the TAC arrives. The point is to practice being the person who does not know the room, and figuring it out anyway.
3. **The assumption delta is the value.** The gap between what you assumed and what the TAC proved is where the learning happens. We document the gap, not just the final map.
