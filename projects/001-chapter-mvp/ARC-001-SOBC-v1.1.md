## CASE 1 — STRATEGIC CASE

### The problem

MEAC student athletes graduate with athletic credentials
and academic transcripts. Neither document answers
the question an employer actually asks:

*Can this person think strategically about my industry?*

NIL programs address income during enrollment.
Career services addresses resume formatting.
Life skills programs address personal development.

None of them produce verifiable evidence of
professional-grade strategic thinking.

The gap is not motivation. MEAC athletes are
among the most disciplined, high-performing
people on any campus. The gap is infrastructure.
No protocol exists to capture and document
the strategic work they are capable of producing.

### The House v. NCAA context

The House v. NCAA settlement creates a $20.5 billion
compliance obligation across Division I athletics.
MEAC institutions face the same mandate as Power Five
programs with a fraction of the compliance budget.

Every compliance investment must do more than one job.

The Exchange satisfies the beyond-NIL professional
development mandate while producing auditable records
that satisfy accreditation requirements — at near-zero
operational cost.

### The strategic position

The Exchange occupies uncontested space.

University career services operates in commodity space —
resume reviews, job boards, interview prep.
NIL compliance tools operate in product space —
disclosure tracking, marketplace management.
Leadership and life skills programs operate in
product space — workshops, speakers, certifications.

None of them produce domain-specific strategic analysis
validated by practitioners who have operated at the
highest levels of their fields.

That space is genesis. Unoccupied. The Exchange
is there first.

### Origin and validation

The Exchange Protocol originates from fourteen years
of documented development:

- 2012: Foot in the Door — internship matching platform,
  Portland, OR. Portfolio-as-credential concept first identified.
- 2013: Student Athlete Exchange (SAX) — first place,
  OSU Startup Weekend. Published in Athletic Management.
  University FTE replacement model identified.
- 2024: GP Monticello field deployment — seven months,
  3,000 pages of structured execution intelligence,
  $1.8M in identified cost savings. Protocol validated
  in live operations.
- 2026: The Exchange Protocol — MEAC MVP deployment.
  ECHO execution intelligence substrate underneath.

## CASE 2 — ECONOMIC CASE

### Cost of the current state

A university career services FTE costs $45,000–65,000
annually in salary alone, before benefits, overhead,
and management cost. Outcomes are measured by
placement rates — a lagging indicator that tells you
what happened, not what the athlete can do.

Employer surveys consistently show that athletic
credentials and academic transcripts do not answer
the questions that matter in a hiring decision.
The matching problem identified in 2012 remains unsolved.

### Benefit of The Exchange

One Exchange chapter produces:

- A portfolio entry per athlete per session —
  inspectable, permanent, employer-facing evidence
  of strategic thinking in a named domain
- A Wardley Map deliverable per partner organization —
  competitive intelligence produced at below-market cost
- A TAC evaluation record — the most credible
  professional reference an athlete has at graduation,
  based on four years of documented work product
- An ETU dataset — structured execution intelligence
  that compounds in value with every session

These outputs replace what a career services FTE
produces, at a fraction of the cost, with outputs
that are verifiable rather than self-reported.

### Measurable outcomes

| Metric | Current state | Exchange target |
|---|---|---|
| Portfolio entries at graduation | 0 | 8–16 per athlete |
| Employer response rate to portfolio | N/A | Measurable by session 2 |
| TAC-to-hire pipeline | None | 1+ per cohort per year |
| Partner org engagement | Ad hoc | 1 per semester minimum |
| Career services cost | $45K–65K FTE | $0–4K protocol cost |

## CASE 3 — COMMERCIAL CASE

### Revenue streams

**Stream 1: University contract**
The Exchange replaces or supplements one career
services FTE function. Contract value: $15,000–35,000
annually per chapter. Offset against FTE cost,
the university saves $10,000–50,000 per year
while producing better outcomes.

**Stream 2: Partner organization engagement fee**
Each partner organization pays an engagement fee
to receive a Wardley Map deliverable produced by
athletes under TAC supervision. Fee: $2,500–7,500
per engagement. One engagement per semester
at MVP stage.

**Stream 3: TAC certification network**
As the protocol scales, TAC certification becomes
a revenue stream. TACs pay a certification fee
to be listed in the Exchange network and gain
access to the pre-vetted talent pipeline.
Fee: $500–1,500 per TAC per year at scale.

**Stream 4: ECHO dataset licensing (4–7 years)**
The structured execution intelligence dataset
generated through every Exchange session becomes
a proprietary training substrate for AI systems
operating in sports business, NIL, and adjacent domains.
This stream activates after sufficient deployment
history. Not a current revenue stream — modeled as
a future option, licensed under terms separate from
the CC and MIT layers per Principle 6.

## CASE 4 — FINANCIAL CASE

> **All figures in this case are estimates.** Setup cost,
> operating cost, cashflow, funding assumptions, and the
> sensitivity scenarios all use estimates derived from
> comparable institutional benchmarks and prior Exchange
> deployment history. None have been validated against
> a specific institutional finance office or an existing
> Exchange contract. Treat as indicative only until the
> chapter's institutional finance office has confirmed
> each line item.

### Setup cost

The Exchange Protocol is MIT-licensed. The governance
repository is public. No platform licensing, no seat
fees, no per-chapter setup charge.

Chapter setup requires:

- One Chapter Lead (existing institutional staff)
- One or more TACs (recruited volunteer)
- One Map Lead (trained institutional staff or student)
- One version-controlled repository (GitHub, free tier)
- One bound participation agreement template
  (legal review, one-time)

No new capital investment. No technology procurement.
No platform contract.

### Operating cost

Per chapter, per academic year:

| Item | Cost | Funding source |
|---|---|---|
| Chapter Lead time (≈4 hrs/week) | Within existing role | Institutional salary |
| Map Lead time (≈10 hrs per session × 8 sessions) | Within existing role or work-study | Institutional / work-study |
| TAC time | $0 (volunteer) | Volunteer |
| Venue | $0 | Institutional space |
| Repository hosting | $0 | GitHub free tier |
| Legal review (initial + annual) | $2,000–4,000 | Institutional legal |
| **Total new institutional spend** | **$2,000–4,000** | Career services budget |

### Cashflow profile

Under the Commercial Case revenue streams 1–3:

- Year 1 university contract revenue: $15,000–35,000
- Year 1 partner organization engagement revenue:
  $2,500–7,500 (one engagement per semester minimum)
- Year 1 incremental institutional cost: $2,000–4,000

The chapter operates net-positive to the institution
in Year 1. Partner-organization fees flow to the
Exchange governance entity to fund protocol
maintenance and ECHO substrate development.

### Funding source

Chapter funding is a **reallocation**, not a new
appropriation. The university contract amount is
offset against existing career services FTE cost
(see Economic Case). No new line item is required
in the institutional budget.

For institutions without an allocated career services
function for athletes, the Exchange can be funded
from existing student development, NIL compliance,
or beyond-NIL professional development budgets
created by the House v. NCAA settlement obligation.

### Affordability

At $15,000–35,000 per chapter per year, against a
$45,000–65,000 FTE cost and a $20.5 billion
settlement compliance obligation across Division I,
the Exchange is affordable at every MEAC institution
and at most Division I institutions beyond MEAC.

### Sensitivity

Three scenarios tested:

1. **University terminates contract mid-year.**
   Chapter continues under volunteer TAC and student
   Map Lead until the academic year ends. Athlete
   portfolios are unaffected per Principle 3 and ADR-002.
2. **TAC volunteer pool shrinks.**
   Principle 2 is non-negotiable. If no qualified TAC
   is available, no session runs. The chapter does
   not degrade quality to maintain cadence.
3. **Partner-organization engagements fall to zero.**
   Chapter continues — partner engagements fund
   protocol governance, not chapter operations.
   Chapter operations are funded by the university
   contract alone.

## CASE 5 — MANAGEMENT CASE

### Delivery roles

| Role | Responsibility | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Chapter Lead | Owns chapter compliance per ARC-001-FRMW. Signs the checklist. | Institutional staff (AD designee, career services director, or equivalent) |
| Map Lead | Runs the session. Captures the SCQA session record. Commits artifacts to repository. | Institutional staff or trained student |
| TAC | Challenges placements in Phases 3 and 4. Co-signs ETU at session close. | Volunteer practitioner with domain currency per NFR-002 |
| Athlete | Produces the map under challenge. Owns portfolio per Principle 3. | Enrolled college athlete per NFR-001 |

### Timeline to Session 1

Ninety days from chapter agreement signing:

- **Day 0.** Chapter agreement and participation
  agreement template reviewed by institutional legal.
- **Day 0–30.** Chapter Lead named. Map Lead
  identified. At least one TAC recruited and
  credentialed per NFR-002.
- **Day 30–60.** Athlete cohort onboarded.
  Participation agreements signed. Wardley Mapping 101
  and ARC-000-BRIEF-v1.0 distributed.
- **Day 60–90.** ARC-001-FRMW signed by Chapter Lead,
  Map Lead, and TAC. Session 1 scheduled.
- **Day 90.** Session 1. First portfolio entry produced
  and committed to repository.

### Governance arrangements

- Governance documents version-controlled in public
  repository per NFR-004.
- Compliance verifiable by any third party without
  founder contact per Principle 5 and the FRMW.
- Risk management per ARC-001-RISK-v1.0 using
  HM Treasury Orange Book scoring.
- Data protection per ARC-000-DPIA-v1.0 covering
  FERPA and GDPR-adjacent principles.
- Architectural decisions logged in ARC-000-ADR-v1.0
  using the Nygard format.

### Success metrics

Mapped to the Economic Case outcome table:

| Metric | Measurement method | Review cadence |
|---|---|---|
| Portfolio entries per athlete | Chapter repository commit count | Per semester |
| TAC currency | Credential document review per NFR-002 and R05 | Annual |
| Partner engagement throughput | Signed partner agreements | Per semester |
| Employer response rate | Chapter outreach logs | Per semester from Session 2 |
| Contract renewal | Institutional renewal decision | Annual |

### Review cadence

- **Per session.** Map Lead commits artifacts,
  including co-signed ETU, within 24 hours of
  session close.
- **Per semester.** Chapter Lead reviews the
  FRMW compliance matrix.
- **Annually.** TAC currency renewal check per R05.
  Chapter agreement renewal with institution.
  Legal review of participation agreement template.
- **Per ADR.** New decisions logged as they arise.
  Superseding ADRs replace prior entries — decisions
  are never amended in place.

### Exit and portability

Chapters may terminate at any time. Termination does
not affect:

- Athlete portfolios (athlete-owned per Principle 3).
- Shared payload hashes (Bitcoin-anchored per ADR-001,
  permanent regardless of chapter status).
- Version-controlled artifacts (public repository
  accessible indefinitely).

An athlete who transfers to another institution can
continue with an Exchange chapter at the new
institution or, where none exists, retain their
portfolio as personal property. No transfer friction.
