# Context Document: Sowell — Knowledge and Decisions

**Source:** Thomas Sowell, *Knowledge and Decisions* (1980)
**Domain:** Political Economy / Information Theory
**Application:** The structural reason why athletic experience is invisible to employers

---

## Core Arguments Extracted for the Exchange Protocol

### 1. Dispersed, Contextual Knowledge
Sowell's central thesis is that knowledge is not a single, aggregate commodity. The most valuable knowledge is dispersed, highly contextual, and time-sensitive. It exists at the edge of the system, held by the person closest to the situation.

**Application to SAX:** The knowledge an athlete builds over 18 years—reading a defensive formation in 0.3 seconds, holding multiple variables under physical stress, subordinating personal interest to mission success—is dispersed knowledge. It is highly contextual to the moment it happens. 

### 2. The Impossibility of Upward Aggregation
Because dispersed knowledge is bound to its context, it cannot be effectively transmitted upward to a central authority without losing its meaning. A central planner (or evaluator) can only process aggregated statistics, not the contextual reality.

**Application to SAX:** This explains why the 49ers cut happened the way it did, and why employers cannot evaluate athletes. The scout watching film or the HR recruiter reading a resume are central evaluators. They can measure the 40-yard dash or read the words "demonstrated leadership," but they cannot access the dispersed knowledge the athlete holds from *inside* the play. The traditional evaluation system is structurally incapable of capturing the athlete's actual capability.

### 3. The Need for Local Verification
If knowledge cannot be aggregated upward, the only way to evaluate it is to create mechanisms that verify it locally, at the point of action.

**Application to SAX:** The Exchange Protocol does exactly this. Instead of asking the athlete to aggregate their experience into a resume (which destroys the context), the protocol asks them to map a new context (the Wardley Map) and records their reasoning in the moment (the ETU). The map and the ETU make the dispersed knowledge legible *without destroying it*, because they capture the athlete's local decision-making process, co-signed by a practitioner who understands the domain.

## The Origin Parallel
The 1997 San Francisco 49ers cut is the lived proof of Sowell's argument. The problem was not a lack of skill; the problem was an information asymmetry. The athlete possessed highly valuable, dispersed knowledge that the centralized evaluation system (the NFL scouting combine and roster management) could not measure or see. 

The Exchange Protocol is the infrastructure built to solve this exact information asymmetry for the transition out of sport.
