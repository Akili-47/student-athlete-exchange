# Source Document: British Army Followership Doctrine Note (2023)
**Context:** The operational definition of the TAC role and the Challenge Spectrum
**Relevance to SAX:** The core mechanism of the Exchange Protocol

## Core Thesis
The British Army Followership Doctrine Note (AC 72029) defines followership as "the act of an individual or individuals willingly accepting the influence of others to achieve a shared outcome." It addresses the critical gap in leadership development: "Leaders matter greatly. But in searching so zealously for better leaders we tend to lose sight of the people these leaders will lead" [1].

## Key Mechanisms

### Values-Based Followership
Followership is not passive obedience. It is an active, values-based commitment to the organization's mission and the leader's intent.

### Servant Followership
The follower's role is to support the leader and the team, prioritizing the shared outcome over personal ambition.

### Followership in Mission Command
Mission Command relies on trust and mutual understanding. Effective followership is essential for this system to function, as followers must be empowered to act independently within the commander's intent.

### The Responsibility to Challenge
A crucial element of exemplary followership is the responsibility to challenge constructively when necessary. This is not insubordination; it is a vital check on the leader's judgment and the organization's direction.

### The Behavioural Spectrum
The doctrine outlines a spectrum of followership behaviours, from passive (requiring constant direction) to exemplary (proactive, independent, and constructive).

## Application to The Exchange Protocol
The Followership Doctrine fundamentally reframes the Exchange Protocol. While existing professional development programs focus on producing leaders, the Exchange aims to produce **exemplary followers**.

This is the exact function of the **TAC (Tactical Advising Coach)**. The TAC's role is not to teach or facilitate, but to provide the constructive challenge that forces the athlete to calibrate their assumptions against operational reality. The athlete learns to operate within a protocol they did not design, challenge constructively, and produce verifiable results.

The Followership Doctrine provides the theoretical foundation for the TAC's role and the "Challenge Spectrum" within the SAX architecture.
