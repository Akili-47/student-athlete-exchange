## 1. Value Chain and Evolution

Wardley Mapping is not a static diagram; it is a visual representation of a value chain plotted against evolution. 

*   **Value Chain (y-axis):** Starts with the visible user need at the top and traces downward through increasingly invisible components required to meet that need.
*   **Evolution (x-axis):** Components evolve from left to right through supply and demand competition: Genesis $\rightarrow$ Custom Built $\rightarrow$ Product (+ rental) $\rightarrow$ Commodity (+ utility).

**Relevance to The Exchange:** This is the core methodology of the session. The athletes are not brainstorming ideas; they are structuring an industry's value chain and plotting its evolutionary state. The TAC's role is to challenge the x-axis placement — where research says "product," operational reality might say "custom."

## 2. Pioneers, Settlers, and Town Planners (PST)

Wardley argues against traditional organizational structures (by type: IT, Finance, Marketing) because they obscure the natural flow of activities from chaotic to linear. Instead, he proposes a structure defined by evolution:

*   **Pioneers:** Operate in the uncharted domain (Genesis). They explore the unknown, build prototypes, and discover what is possible. They are comfortable with high failure rates.
*   **Settlers:** Operate in the transitional domain (Custom/Product). They take the prototypes built by Pioneers and turn them into scalable, profitable products. They build trust and understanding.
*   **Town Planners:** Operate in the industrialized domain (Commodity). They take the products and turn them into standardized, high-volume utilities. They focus on efficiency and metrics.

**Relevance to The Exchange (The Rotation Model):** 
This is the breakthrough for athlete development. In a football team, an athlete might be a specialized left tackle for four years. But in an Exchange chapter, athletes rotate through the PST roles across different sessions:
1.  **Session A (Pioneer):** The athlete leads the mapping of a completely new, uncharted domain where no clear rules exist. They practice leading in uncertainty.
2.  **Session B (Settler):** The athlete takes an existing map and operationalizes it for a specific partner. They practice translating concepts into workable products.
3.  **Session C (Town Planner):** The athlete serves as the Map Lead, ensuring strict adherence to the SCQA protocol and FR-000 through FR-006 requirements. They practice strict compliance and efficiency.

Just like West Point cadets rotate through leadership and followership roles in the barracks, Exchange athletes rotate through PST cognitive states. They practice leading and following *in context*.

## 3. Doctrine vs. Gameplay

Wardley distinguishes between three types of patterns:
*   **Climatic Patterns:** Things that will happen regardless of your choices (e.g., components will evolve).
*   **Doctrine:** Universally useful principles you should always apply (e.g., focus on user needs, use appropriate methods, remove duplication).
*   **Gameplay:** Context-specific strategic moves you can choose to make *once you can read the map* (e.g., open source plays, talent raids, fear/uncertainty/doubt).

**Relevance to The Exchange:**
The Exchange Protocol itself is Doctrine. It is universally applicable across any university and any athletic program. 

The output of an Exchange session (the ETU) is the discovery of Gameplay. When the TAC corrects the athletes' map, they are revealing the actual gameplay happening in the industry — the moves that are invisible to outsiders but obvious to practitioners. The athlete graduates not just with a degree, but with a documented understanding of how to play the game in their chosen field.
